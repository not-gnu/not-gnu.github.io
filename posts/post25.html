self-destructing.

gnu • june 23, 2026

We were eating Lanzhou Noodles just before noon. Marvin was going on about the friends he’ll meet later in the day. He usually dedicates his two rest days meeting friends and catching-up with them. He does a lot for his friends—even for me too. He drives his friends to places no matter how far it is, he makes sure to catch-up with people over a cup of coffee, and many others.

I asked him why he’s so dedicated. He said, “gotta be there for the folks, you know?”

“Anything you like talking about with them?” I asked.

“I mostly talk to people about their life. Some are facing some mental health stuff, and I make sure I’m there, you know.”

Funnily enough, just a day before; I briefly almost broke-down during service while washing the dishes. Maybe it was because it was a slight correction Vinnie told me with regards to a food I made. It wasn’t that intense, very minor—calm even; but it was enough to spiral my mind out of control because I started question my worth. I started reflecting on my life, my circumstances, and the fact that I’m in my thoughts all alone.

Suddenly, just after 1-2 minutes after breaking-down, it stopped. It looked like I was a completely different person. I completely forgot what I was breaking-down for and started working as efficiently as I can in the kitchen once again.

I told Marvin about this just after he mentioned about “being there.”

“Ah, so you’re the strong type.” Marvin said.

“What do you mean?”

“You’re able to bounce back right away. Not many people can do that.”

I mean, yeah I guess so. I guess it’s just part of me growing-up all alone as a single child: I have to handle everything myself including my feelings. I never learned to rely on anyone. Ever.

“But you know, they say the even strongest people are those who can be openly vulnerable in-front of someone.” Marvin added.

I agree with it wholeheartedly actually. I started reflecting when did I actually become absolutely “open” to someone so much that I told them everything that’s in my thoughts. Probably never.

I didn’t add anything more to the conversation after that—nor did I open up, or even consider so in the future. But it was a nice point of reflection I guess.

I thought about the relationships I had with people: my family, my friends, and the women I considered before. Suddenly, I find myself in a dilemma of what it actually means to “connect” with someone.

I’m a deeply distrustful person. During High School, I did everything myself because 99% of my batch mates were absolute trash. I never learned to trust my parents because one happens to be dishonest with his wife, and the other had disappointed me too much which I really hate saying because she had gave me so much love regardless of the fact. Most of my friends never stayed, nor have I really connected with anyone on a personal level. Heck, only one person outside my family remembered and greeted me on my birthday even (lol.)

Because of this, I started questioning my significance and if I really “matter” to people. When it comes to love interests, I think it’s an even more complicated. Sometimes, I do think some of the people I’ve had feelings for did care; but I never actually cared or shown to have cared because I was scared, or I’m not used to it.

There was one girl who was basically my “lockdown” buddy. Honestly, I don’t think I would have ever survived those two-years of lockdown without her. Anyway, there are facets of my life I told her, and part of me thinks that she was starting to care—and feelings started showing-up. But as I was about to end college, the surmounting pressure of what the fuck I was going to do after college was pressuring me so much that it broke me. I didn’t tell her anything about those feelings I had. She really tried reaching out, asking why my camera was off in our group work meetings, why it’s taking me days to reply back to her messages, asking me what’s wrong, I was even touched she remembered my birthday; but I’m almost always depressed during my birthday so it took me two-days before I could thank her.

I was really selfish, admittedly. I never told her how graduation was something I’m not looking for; that I wouldn’t be able to join her in medical school. That let’s say we did become a thing in the future, how we’re going to make it work out because I didn’t fucking know what I was going to do in my life. I didn’t know what I can provide for someone. I was scared of who I am. I was scared that I’m not fit for that kind of thing, so I silently laid low, and her interest in me slowly diminished.

These patterns of self-loathing were really hard to navigate (heck, even recounting these while writing is so hard to make sense of.) Yet again, this persistence is without merit.

There was another girl in my first year whom I was probably the most open with—vulnerability-wise. The conversation got really deep to the point that she had fought with her parents, and she turned to me and told me she wanted to end herself. She knew were her mom hid her gun. I tried comforting her as much as I can—I had forgotten exactly how, but maybe I just told her about how I also wanted to do it too, then I went into depths about my parents as well. She replied, “oh you actually have it much worse than me.”

But at that point, I felt disgusted with myself. Of course, because she told me that, I went through great lengths of care such as meeting her every morning to bring food, etc. and making sure her parents weren’t asses, but the more I took care, the less I looked at myself.

I relied too much on her solidarity that I’ve lost my sense of identity. I say this not in the sense of “losing myself,” but in the sense of what she “deserves.” She deserves someone who didn’t say “oh I wish I killed myself too.” I didn’t want to drag her down with my self-loathing. Anyway, a guy she liked who was 100x better than me started flirting with her, so I let her go haphazardly.

That was probably the closest I can say I was “bare” to someone.

Now I look back to what Marvin says about “the strongest people are the most vulnerable.” Is it strong if being vulnerable entails a degree of self-righteousness that you selfishly expect someone to just “fix” you, or is it more righteous to not drag people with your problems and just fix them yourself?

For my entire life, I had probably chose the latter. Now I feel my life had been devoid of any meaning and significant connections because I had chose to let go of people who probably had cared. I feel that my life has no meaning because I refuse to have any deep conversation with anyone, even to the company and friends I have right now.

As I write this conclusion, I admit that I’m still struggling the right answer, or the right insight to end this prompt.

As much as I think I am able to process my emotions on my own; I can’t. I admit, much like Carmy from the Bear, I drown myself in the stresses of my career and the kitchen to avoid dealing with my inherited fate, these sort of thoughts, any form of intimacy, and deep connections.

I can’t see a future where I am with someone anymore. I even find dying at 30 to be such a peaceful thought to process.

Ok, I think I’m self-destructing now lol. I really can’t think of any fruitful antithesis to my reflection. This blog was supposed to exist to provide clarity against my turbulent thoughts; to put into words things that I would otherwise fail to say to other people when it matters.

I think my mind needs a break.

Maybe someday, I’ll get more clarity—hopefully.
